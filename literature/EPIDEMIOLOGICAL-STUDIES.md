# Epidemiological Studies: Lithium and Dementia

## Overview

This document summarizes population-level studies examining the association between naturally occurring lithium exposure and dementia incidence.

---

## Key Studies

### 1. Denmark National Study (2017)

**Citation**: Kessing LV, Gerds TA, Knudsen NN, et al. Association of Lithium in Drinking Water With the Incidence of Dementia. *JAMA Psychiatry*. 2017;74(10):1005-1010.

| Parameter | Value |
| --- | --- |
| **Design** | Nationwide, population-based, nested case-control |
| **Population** | All Danish residents aged 50-90 |
| **Cases** | 73,731 with dementia diagnosis |
| **Controls** | 733,653 (10:1 matched by age/sex) |
| **Exposure** | Mean lithium in drinking water by municipality |
| **Period** | 1970-2013 (diagnoses: 1995-2013) |

#### Exposure Distribution

- Range: 0.3 - 30.7 µg/L
- Categories: 2.0-5.0, 5.1-10.0, 10.1-15.0, >15.0 µg/L

#### Results

| Lithium Level (µg/L) | IRR (95% CI) | p-value | Interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2.0-5.0 | 1.00 (reference) | — | Baseline |
| 5.1-10.0 | **1.22** (1.19-1.25) | <.001 | 22% ↑ risk |
| 10.1-15.0 | 0.98 (0.96-1.01) | .17 | No difference |
| >15.0 | **0.83** (0.81-0.85) | <.001 | 17% ↓ risk |

#### Key Findings

1. **Nonlinear (U-shaped) relationship** between lithium and dementia
2. **Highest exposure protective**: 17% reduced risk at >15 µg/L
3. **Middle exposure paradox**: Slightly increased risk at 5-10 µg/L
4. **Consistent across dementia types**: Similar patterns for AD and vascular dementia

#### Denmark Study Limitations

- Observational design (cannot prove causation)
- Ecological fallacy (population-level exposure assigned to individuals)
- Potential confounding by other geographic factors
- No individual-level lithium measurement

### 2. Denmark Bipolar Disorder Registry Study (2008)

**Citation**: Kessing LV, Søndergård L, Forman JL, Andersen PK. Lithium treatment and risk of dementia. *Arch Gen Psychiatry*. 2008;65(11):1331-5.

| Parameter | Value |
| --- | --- |
| **Design** | Nationwide registry-based cohort |
| **Population** | ~5,000 patients with bipolar disorder |
| **Exposure** | Lithium treatment (vs. other medications) |
| **Comparison** | Anticonvulsants, antidepressants, antipsychotics |
| **Outcome** | Dementia diagnosis |

#### Bipolar Study Results

| Medication | Association with Dementia |
| --- | --- |
| **Lithium** | **Protective** (statistically significant) |
| Anticonvulsants | No association |
| Antidepressants | No association |
| Antipsychotics | No association |

#### Key Finding

Lithium was the **only medication** associated with reduced dementia risk in bipolar patients.

#### Bipolar Study Limitations

- Possible confounding by bipolar severity
- Selection bias (healthier patients may receive lithium)
- No dose-response analysis

### 3. United States Groundwater Study (2018)

**Citation**: Parker WF, Gorges RJ, Gao YN, et al. Association Between Groundwater Lithium and the Diagnosis of Bipolar Disorder and Dementia in the United States. *JAMA Psychiatry*. 2018;75(7):751-754.

| Parameter | Value |
| --- | --- |
| **Design** | Cross-sectional, ecological |
| **Location** | United States (county-level) |
| **Exposure** | Groundwater lithium levels |
| **Outcomes** | Bipolar disorder and dementia rates |

#### US Study Results

- Counties with higher groundwater lithium had **lower dementia rates**
- Association consistent with Danish findings
- Effect size similar to Denmark study

#### US Study Limitations

- Ecological design
- Cross-sectional (no temporal relationship)
- County-level analysis less precise than individual

### 4. Texas Drinking Water Study (2014)

**Citation**: Blüml V, et al. Lithium in the public water supply and suicide mortality in Texas. *J Psychiatr Res*. 2013;47(3):407-11.

While this study focused on **suicide** rather than dementia, it established:
- Geographic variation in lithium levels across Texas counties
- Inverse association between lithium levels and suicide rates
- Supports biological plausibility of trace lithium effects

## Biological Plausibility

### Why Trace Lithium Might Matter

The Nature 2025 study provided mechanistic explanation:

1. **Endogenous brain lithium** is 1000x lower than therapeutic levels
2. **Even trace amounts** have biological activity
3. **GSK3β inhibition** occurs at very low concentrations
4. **Microglia modulation** detectable at micro-levels
5. **Cumulative effects** of long-term exposure

### Dose-Response Considerations

| Level | Source | Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| < 5 µg/L | Drinking water | Possible deficiency |
| 5-15 µg/L | Drinking water | Variable |
| > 15 µg/L | Drinking water | Protective |
| 0.25-0.5 mEq/L | Low-dose therapy | Cognitive benefit in trials |
| 0.6-1.2 mEq/L | Therapeutic | Bipolar treatment |

## Policy Implications

### Should Lithium Be Added to Water Supplies?

**Arguments For**:
- Population-level intervention
- Low cost
- Evidence of benefit

**Arguments Against**:
- Individual variation in optimal dose
- Cannot consent entire population
- Environmental concerns
- U-shaped curve suggests unpredictable effects
- Better to target high-risk individuals

### Current Consensus
**No public health agency recommends water supplementation.** Instead, research focuses on targeted supplementation for at-risk individuals.

Populations in low-lithium regions may be at higher baseline risk for dementia—but this is speculative and requires confirmation.

## Outstanding Questions

1. **Causality**: Do epidemiological associations reflect causal relationships?
2. **Mechanism**: Is the protective effect from lithium itself or correlated factors?
3. **Threshold**: What is the minimum protective level?
4. **Supplementation**: Can individual supplementation replicate population effects?
5. **Formulation**: Does water lithium differ from supplement lithium?
